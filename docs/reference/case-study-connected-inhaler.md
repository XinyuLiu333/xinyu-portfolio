# Connected Inhaler Management — Case Study Content

**Source:** Xinyu Liu's Recent Work_Google 2019.pdf (pages 5-31)
**Company:** Teva Pharmaceuticals
**Role:** Design Lead
**Team:** 3 designers, 18 months

## The Problem

Many respiratory patients (Asthma & COPD) are inadequately uncontrolled:
- 20.1% controlled
- 34.8% partially controlled
- 45.1% uncontrolled (per GINA guidelines, European REALISE survey)

Many "real" respiratory patients have poor adherence to therapy and clinically significant technique errors. Poor adherence to preventative therapy and poor inhaler technique are both linked to poor outcomes.

With years of experience in respiratory health, Teva was deeply aware of these existing issues. Teva had been developing a connected inhaler ecosystem to address patients' poor adherence and poor technique issues. Before project kickoff, Teva already had several inhaler apps under development.

## Business Objectives

An app for each connected inhaler approach created an isolated and disjointed view for patients, increasing their effort to manage medications and identify correlations. Feature duplication increased effort for Teva to manage and keep things consistent.

Developing a consolidated connected inhaler management system was a win-win for every stakeholder.

## Vision: What Would It Look Like?

**Hassle-free medication management and education experience for patients**

Persona: Janet, age 29, moderate asthma patient using both ProAir Digihaler and AirDuo Digihaler.

**With consolidation:**
- Monitoring triangulated insights from multiple inhalers in one place
- Getting consolidated environmental notifications and dose reminders at the right time
- Sharing one report with doctor during check-up

**Without consolidation:**
- Too many duplicated notifications buzzing in the morning
- Jumping between apps to answer doctor's questions
- "Which app should I go for checking my data from the red inhaler?"

## Research: Jobs-to-be-Done

After several rounds of ethnographic research and contextual inquiry, the team developed a set of asthma patient Jobs-to-be-Done:

**Patient quotes:**
- "I want to feel normal"
- "I don't want to feel afraid of something bad happening/going to the ER"
- "I don't want to be seen as a lesser person because of my asthma"
- "I want to be in control"

### Sample JTBD: Avoiding the Unexpected (1.1)
- **Context:** After having bad days
- **Job:** I want to know why I feel good on some days and bad on others
- **Desired outcome:** So that I can take action to avoid triggers and manage asthma with confidence
- **Constraints:** Symptoms and triggers are unpredictable; chronic symptoms fluctuate and are not always recognized as asthma; struggle to recognize triggers, often learn only after trial-and-error with many exacerbations

### Sample JTBD: Peace of Mind (2.1)
- **Context:** When experiencing a minor exacerbation
- **Job:** I want my rescue inhaler to work efficiently. I want to know I'm getting better.
- **Desired outcome:** Live with reassurance/confidence that I know what to do; continue what I was doing
- **Constraints:** Forget where I put it; can't take a deep breath; don't know for sure if I use it correctly; no device consistency requires frequent training; "mixing up controller and rescue inhaler is a BIG problem" (from HCP)

### Sample JTBD: Peace of Mind (2.2)
- **Context:** When prescribed a controller medication
- **Job:** I need to know why/when I need to take it
- **Desired outcome:** Know if I take it correctly it will help my condition
- **Constraints:** Intention-Action Gap — stopped using controller once feeling better; lack of perceived feedback for controller vs. rescue; "it's just bad to rely on an inhaler"; forget sometimes; meds have different schedules; worried about side effects

### Sample JTBD: Peace of Mind (2.5)
- **Context:** When not feeling better
- **Job:** I want to know why or if I should try a new Rx
- **Desired outcome:** Want to take the right medication
- **Constraints:** "Never thought about having 'goals' around my asthma"; unaware of poor technique

### Sample JTBD: Sense of Self (3.2)
- **Context:** When experiencing a minor exacerbation; starting a new inhaler type
- **Job:** I want my inhaler to be easy to use
- **Desired outcome:** Can continue what I was doing
- **Constraints:** No device consistency requires frequent training; multiple inhalers need different techniques; devices need cleaning; nebulizers require multiple steps

## Mapping Exercises

The design team started looking for opportunity areas based on research insights. They looked into each area of the asthma journey to gain deep understanding of patients' lives and extract opportunities for change and improvement.

Exercises conducted:
- Patient Needs Mapping
- Patient Journey Mapping

## Prioritized Opportunities

Derived from insights and mapping analysis as a north star for concept development:

1. Help me build confidence around my inhaler use so I feel certain and in control every time I use my inhalers.
2. Help me track symptoms and solutions so I can figure out what's working and what's not.
3. Help me identify my triggers so I can take actions to avoid asthma attacks.
4. Help me communicate asthma experiences to my doctor so we develop mutual trust and find an optimal solution together.
5. Help my network easily understand and support me so I don't shoulder this alone.

## Concept Generation

Fully explored the problem space and ideated 50+ initial concepts, refined and narrowed to the most robust ones for field testing. Worked with researchers and brought 5 concepts into user research sessions with asthma sufferers.

## User Research Insights (Round 1)

**Contextual Insights:** Participants most interested in insights (especially rescue inhaler) when they are personal, relevant, informative, and specific to their conditions — providing validation to their understanding of asthma.

**Daily Self-Assessment:** Mixed preferences about entering how they feel. Manual tracking only done when convenient, can be sent directly to doctor, and reduces mental load.

**Data:** Participants see value in usage and technique data if easy to understand and quick to glance through. Less interested in detailed data — only interested during doctor visits. Prefer weekly insights. Expect more information about potential triggers. Integration with environmental data highly desirable.

> "Past 7 days [instead of 30 days] is more helpful for me - it's immediate and it's a good time to see data change positively to tell me something." — P1

## Data Visualization Refinement

Key questions driving refinement:
- What information is most valuable to each group?
- What role will the visuals play in this dialogue?
- What action(s) do we want to incite?

Two weeks of iterations on data visualization concepts covering:
- Self-assessment views
- Inhaler event tracking (ProAir and AirDuo Digihalers)
- Weekly and monthly data views
- Technique quality indicators (Good/Fair/Low/No inhalation)
- Environmental trigger integration

## User Research Insights (Round 2)

**Home:** Participants liked content and presentation on home screen. Additional environment information well received.

**Data:** Participants gravitated towards written-out data over visualizations. Expected visualizations to be more interactive/"clickable." Difficulty interpreting "Fair/Low/No" inhalation events — recommended adding definitions.

## Design Refinement

Key design principle: "As the eyes move from one section to another, the constancy of the design allows the user to focus on changes in the data rather than on changes in the design."

Applied "Overview First, Zoom and Filter, then Details on Demand" — presenting high-level overview of inhaler usage over past 7 days with easy-to-digest visuals showing taken doses, missed doses, and inhaler technique.

## Onboarding Service Blueprint

Mapped the complete new user onboarding flow:
- System layer, App layer, User layer
- Stages: General → First Impression → Initial Use → Ongoing Use
- Emotional journey: Skeptical → Clarified → Excited → Confident → Trusting → Accomplished

Design guidelines:
- Bring teachable moments contextually, over time
- Help users learn while in the moment, not all at once
- Give before take
- Set expectations
- Explicitly communicate value
- Learning by doing
